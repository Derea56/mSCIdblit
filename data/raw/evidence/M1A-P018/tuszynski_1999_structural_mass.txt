Human Spinal Cord Retains Substantial Structural Mass in
Chronic Stages After Injury
MARK H. TUSZYNSKI,12 KATIE GABRIEL,1 KEVIN GERHARDT,2
and SUZANNE SZOLLAR2
ABSTRACT
In chronic stages of human spinal cord injury, atrophy of the cord has been reported in regions
both at and distant to the injury site. Local cord atrophy results from the direct effects of bony im-
pact and ischemia, whereas distant atrophy results from anterograde (Wallerian) and retrograde
axonal degeneration. However, the actual extent of degenerative changes in the chronically injured
human spinal cord both at and remote from the injury site has rarely been reported, and has not
been rigorously quantified to date. In the present study, we quantified the extent of spinal cord at-
rophy in 12 humans with chronic injury (2-34 years posttrauma) utilizing quantitative stereologi-
cal assessment of spinal cord magnetic resonance images, and compared the results to uninjured
human spinal cords. Focal cystic atrophy of the cord, characterized by signal attenuation on Tl-
weighted images, was regularly present at the actual site of impact injury and replaced a mean lon-
gitudinal area equaling less than one spinal cord segment in length (2.01± 0.60 cm2, or a loss of
89.3 ± 17.4% of the longitudinal area of one spinal cord segment). Spinal cord segments immedi-
ately rostral to the zone of cystic degeneration showed atrophy of only 19.4 ± 7.5 % of normal cord
longitudinal area, and spinal cord segments immediately caudal to the zone of cystic degeneration
showed atrophy of 16.5 ±4.1% of normal cord longitudinal area. Extensive spinal cord atrophy ex-
tending beyond the region of injury occurred in two of twelve cases (16.7%), and both were caused
by late syrinx formation. Thus, spinal cord atrophy after trauma remains primarily restricted to
the original site of injury. Experimental neural repair strategies should take into account the im-
portance of "bridging" relatively short zones of cystic atrophy, then promoting axonal regeneration
through potentially long segments of remaining cord parenchyma.
Key words: atrophy; chronic; MRI; regeneration; spinal cord injury; stereology

Spinal cord atrophy distant to the injury site also occurs,
due to anterograde (Wallerian) and retrograde axonal de-

Yet spinal cord injury rarely completely transects the
spinal cord, and electrophysiological transmission across
injury sites has been demonstrated in humans with sub-
stantial neurological impairment after spinal cord injury.
Indeed, the function of these maintained projections can
be transiently augmented by conduction-enhancing com-
pounds such as 4-aminopyridine (Hayes, 1994; Hanse-
boutetal., 1993; Young, 1992; Blight et al.,1991). Patho-
logical studies also frequently reveal a persistent rim of
spinal cord parenchyma between rostral and caudal seg-
ments of the injured spinal cord, and the sprouting of
some neural systems, including sensory projections, into
injured regions (Melinek et al.,1994).Thus, both struc-
tural and electrophysiological evidence indicates the per-
sistence of some neural systems across sites of spinal cord
injury, although these systems may be nonfunctional or
partially functional. Clearly, our knowledge regarding the
full spectrum of anatomical, physiological and functional
alterations that result from spinal cord injury is incom-
plete.
The purpose of the present study was to more fully un-
derstand the structural consequences of chronic human
spinal cord injury. Previous studies have described a
diminution in local and distant spinal cord mass in
chronic stages after human spinal cord injury, but detailed
quantification of the extent of this loss is lacking (Bunge
et al.,1993; Becerra et al.,1995; Taneichi et al.,1994;
Nidecker et al.,1991). Thus, in the present study the ex-
tent of atrophie change in the chronically injured human
spinal cord was determined. Spinal longitudinal areas
were objectively quantified using stereological methods
(Coggeshall, 1992) on magnetic resonance images
(MRIs) of chronically injured human spinal cords at in-
jured segments and in spinal cord regions rostral and cau-
dal to the injury site. Results were compared to MRIs of
uninjured human spinal cords at similar spinal segments.
METHODS
MRI scans of 20 patients were examined. Twelve pa-
tients who had suffered acute mid-cervical spinal cord
compressive injuries at least two years earlier were com-
pared to eight uninjured controls (Table 1).The mean age
of the injured patients was 46 ± 3 years, and that of con-
trol (uninjured) patients was 52 ± 6 years. Eleven of the
12 injured patients were male, and six of the eight con-
trol patients were male. To standardize subject sampling,
patients with lesions outside the mid-cervical region were
not included in the analysis. Scans were performed on a
General Electric 1.5 tesla Clinical Image MRI (General
Electric, Milwaukee, WI) with a TR interval of 500-700,
TE of 12-17, and parasagittal slice thickness of 3 mm.

All measurements were obtained from the most cen-
tral and medial parasagittal MRI slice in each subject.
The following parameters were assessed: (1) the area of
spinal cord atrophy at the original lesion site, (2) the lon-
gitudinal area of spinal cord immediately rostral to the
lesion site over three spinal segments in lesioned sub-
jects, or in three spinal segments rostral to the C4-5 in-
tervertebral disc space in uninjured subjects; and (3) the
longitudinal area of spinal cord immediately caudal to the
lesion site over three spinal segments in lesioned sub-
jects, or in three spinal segments caudal to the C4-5 in-
tervertebral disc space in uninjured subjects. Spinal cord
lesion margins were recognized by diminished MR sig-
nal characteristics on Tl-weighted images and enhanced
signal characteristics in T2-weighted images.
Spinal cord areas were quantified in each subject in
the parasagittal plane using quantitative stereological
methods (Coggeshall, 1992).Stereological methods uti-
lize an "optical dissector" (a standard reference sampling
plane in space) to obtain an unbiased estimate of sample
size for an identified anatomical structure as follows (for
review, see Peterson and Jones, 1993): a regular grid of
crosses on an acetyl acetate sheet with in intergrid dis-
tance of 5 mm was superimposed on a X 10 magnified
image of MR images from each patient. The number of
crosses intersecting the spinal cord in each defined re-
gion was summated, converted to actual area measure-
ments according to the appropriate distance and magni-
fication factors (X10), and expressed in mm2. Area
measurements for each subject rostral to the injured site
were divided by mean area measurements in the same
sampling region rostral to C4-5 in the uninjured group.
Similarly, area measurements for each subject caudal to
the injured site were divided by mean area measurements
in the same sampling region caudal to C4-5 in the unin-
jured group.
Area measurements and height measurements were
compared between the injured and intact group using un-
paired, one-tailed Student's t test. Data are presented as
mean ± SEM.

Spinal Cord Atrophy at the Lesion Site
The spinal cord lesion site was identifiable as a dis-
tinct region of MRI signal attenuation typically corre-
sponding closely to an anterior dissociation of vertebral
body integrity (Fig. 1). The average area of this spinal
cord lesion site in the 10 lesioned subjects, quantified uti-
lizing the stereological methods noted above, was 2.01 ±
0.60 cm2. In comparison, the average area of one intact
segment of the cervical spinal cord spanning a region
from the mid-C4 to mid-C5 disc space was 2.25 ± 0.44
cm2 in uninjured subjects. Thus, the mean area of signal
attenuation corresponding to atrophy from a chronic
spinal cord injury equaled 89.3% of the longitudinal area
of one normal spinal cord segment.
Spinal Cord Atrophy Rostral and Caudal to the
Lesion Site
Average spinal cord area rostral to the injury site mea-
sured 5.31 ± 0.49cm2 (±SEM) in injured individuals and
6.61 ±0.35 cm2 in uninjured controls, a difference that was
statistically significant (p < 0.05;Table 1,Fig.2A).Cau-
dal to the injury site, average spinal cord areas also differed
significantly, measuring 5.35 ± 0.26cm2 (±SEM) in in-
jured individuals and 6.42± 0.24cm2 in uninjured sub-
jects (p < 0.005;Table 1,Fig.2B).However, the magni-
tude of difference in area measurements between the two
groups was not substantial: injured subjects demonstrated
a loss of mean spinal longitudinal area of only 19.4 ± 7.5%
compared to uninjured subjects rostral the lesion site, and
16.5 ±4.1% caudal to the injury site compared to unin-
jured subjects. Stated differently, uninjured subjects re-
tained a mean of 80.6% of normal spinal cord longitudinal
area rostral to the injury site, and retained 83.5% of nor-
mal area caudal to the injury site (Fig.3A,B).
Correlation of Spinal Cord Atrophy to
Frankel Grade
The severity of neurological deficit (Frankel grade)
generally correlated with the extent of spinal cord atro-
phy at the lesion site (Fig. 4),although the number of
subjects in each group was insufficient to generate sig-
nificant statistical correlations.

restricted in degree. The mean amount of cystic atrophy at
the actual spinal cord injury site replaces less than one lon-
gitudinal cord segment. Further, the degree of atrophy ros-
tral and caudal to sites immediately adjoining the injury
site is also relatively modest in degree: approximately 80%
of normal spinal cord longitudinal area is retained in spinal
cord segments adjacent to injury sites an average of 15
years after injury. Atrophy at the injury site is caused by
direct tissue damage with secondary necrosis and cavita-
tion, whereas anterograde and retrograde axonal degener-
ation is likely to account for spinal cord atrophy in spinal
cord segments adjacent to the injury (Ramon y Cajal, 1928;
Bunge et al.,1993; Becerra et al.,1995; Taneichi et al.,
1994; Nidecker et al.,1991). Extensive atrophy remote
from the injury site occurs uncommonly and can be at-
tributed to posttraumatic syrinx formation (Bunge et al.,
1993; Curati et al.,1992; Tosi et al.,1993; Ditunno and
Formal, 1994;Nidecker et al.,1991).
Spinal cord damage from an acute compressive injury
is caused by several factors: (1) direct neuronal and ax-
onal destruction from mechanical disruption by
compressive forces, (2) ischemia, (3) posttraumatic/
postanoxic release of excitotoxins, free radicals and cal-
cium, (4) edema, (5) mediators of inflammatory re-
sponses, and (6) delayed apoptosis, or programmed cell
death (Narayan et al.,1996; Zivin and Choi, 1991; Kaku-
las and Taylor, 1992; Young, 1992; Choi, 1988; Hall et
al., 1989; Yum and Faden,- 1990; Crowe et al.,1997).
Thus, what begins as a relatively restricted injury may
enlarge in extent in subacute and chronic phases of in-
jury. Several therapeutic efforts in acute spinal cord in-
jury are directed at preventing the severity of secondary
events that contribute to injury (Zivin and Choi, 1991;
Young, 1992; Choi, 1988; Hall et al.,1989; Yum and
Faden, 1990; Narayan et al.,1996).
Few options are currently available for the treatment
of chronic spinal cord injury. Experimental efforts di-
rected at enhancing axonal impulse conduction through
residual spinal pathways after chronic injury are under
examination in both animals and humans, using agents
such as 4-aminopyridine (Young, 1992; Blight et al.,
1991). Other experimental strategies in animal models
have attempted to promote axonal regrowth and regen-
eration, although primarily in acute injury models
(Schnell and Schwab, 1990; Bregman et al., 1995;
Schnell et al., 1994; Cheng et al.,1996; Reier et al.,
1992a,b; Young, 1992). Means of promoting regenera-
tion have included the provision of ( 1) supportive cellu-
lar, biological and artificial axonal growth substrates
(Gage et al.,1988; Richardson et al.,1980; Houle and
Johnson, 1989; Yakovleff et al.,1989; Kromer et al.,
1981; Marchand and Woerly, 1990; Hoovler and
Wrathall, 1991; de la Torre, 1982; Gelderd and Quarles,
1990; Tessler, 1991; Himes et al.,1994; Li, Raisman,
1993; Foster et al.,1990; Duncan et al.,1988; Paino and
Bunge, 1991); (2) neuronal growth factors (Houle and
Johnson, 1989; Houle, 1992; Tuszynski et al., 1996,
1997; Nakahara et al.,1996; Schnell et al.,1994;Xu et
al.,1995; Cheng et al.,1996; Menei et al.,1997; Grill et
al., 1997b); (3) blockade of myelin-associated axon
growth inhibitors (Schwab, 1990; McKerracher et al.,
1994;Bregman et al.,1995); or (4)combinations of these
approaches (Tuszynski and Gage, 1995; Schnell et al.,
1994). These experimental manipulations have only
rarely been examined in models of chronic spinal cord
injury, however (Reier et al.,1988; Houle and Reier,
1988; Houle, 1991; Anderson et al.,1991; Houle, 1992;
Houle and Ye, 1997; Houle et al.,1996; Grill et al,
1997a).
Findings from the present study support the testing of
these experimental paradigms in chronic animal injury
models for two reasons: first, loss of spinal cord mass re-
mains primarily restricted to the original injury site in
chronic injury, thus "bridging" growth strategies which
attempt to induce axonal growth across relatively short
zones of parenchymal destruction are appropriate. This
short-distance bridging should then be followed by ma-
nipulations of the injury model that promote axonal
growth through longer segments of remaining cord
parenchyma. Second, the transected proximal tips of ax-
ons remain adjacent to injured regions for extended time
periods after injury (McBride et al., 1990; Li and Rais-
man, 1995), unless injury occurs at high cervical levels,
and these chronically injured axons remain responsive to
neurotrophic factors (Houle and Ye, 1997; Houle et al.,
1996; Grill et al., 1997a). Thus regenerative axonal
growth after chronic spinal cord injury is potentially re-
cruitable. Since the majority of the 250,000 Americans
with spinal cord injuries are in the chronic stages of in-
jury (Stovers and Fine, 1986; Ditunno and Formal, 1994),
further research into chronic injury models could be of
potential relevance to a substantial number of patients.
